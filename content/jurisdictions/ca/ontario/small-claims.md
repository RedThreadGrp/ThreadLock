<!-- SECTION: overview -->
Ontario Small Claims Court is a branch of the Superior Court of Justice and handles civil money disputes up to $35,000 CAD — the highest small claims limit in Canada. It is designed to be accessible to individuals and businesses without requiring a lawyer, though parties may choose to retain legal representation. Common cases include unpaid invoices, security deposit disputes, property damage, and breach of contract. <!-- SECTION: who_can_file -->
Any individual, business, corporation, or other legal entity may file a claim in Ontario Small Claims Court. There is no restriction on legal representation — parties may represent themselves, hire a lawyer, or be represented by a licensed paralegal. Ontario is the only Canadian province with a regulated paralegal profession, making affordable legal help widely available for small claims matters. Claims must be filed in the court location nearest to where the defendant lives, carries on business, or where the event giving rise to the claim occurred.

<!-- SECTION: process -->
To file a claim in Ontario, the plaintiff completes a Plaintiff's Claim (Form 7A) and files it at the Small Claims Court location. The filing fee is CAD $102 for claims up to $1,000 and CAD $229 for claims between $1,000 and $35,000. The court issues the claim and serves a copy on the defendant, or the plaintiff may arrange service independently using a process server.

The defendant has 20 days from service to file a Defence (Form 9A). If the defendant has a counterclaim against the plaintiff, they file a Defendant's Claim (Form 10A) as well. If no defence is filed, the plaintiff may request a default judgment.

Ontario's small claims process includes a Settlement Conference before the trial date. A deputy judge meets with the parties to identify the issues, encourage settlement, and narrow the matters in dispute. Most cases settle at or before the Settlement Conference. If the case proceeds to trial, both parties present evidence and testimony before a deputy judge. Decisions are typically rendered in writing within 45 days of the trial.

<!-- SECTION: evidence_and_prep -->
Key evidence in Ontario small claims cases includes written contracts, invoices, receipts, bank records, photographs, text messages, emails, and witness statements. Ontario judges and deputy judges value organized, coherent presentations — a clear written summary of the dispute submitted before the Settlement Conference can significantly strengthen a claimant's position.

<!-- SECTION: faq -->
Q: Do I need a lawyer for Ontario Small Claims Court?
A: No. Self-representation is common and the court provides self-help resources. You may also use a licensed paralegal, which is often more affordable than a lawyer for matters within the small claims limit.

Q: What is the limitation period for small claims in Ontario?
A: Ontario's basic limitation period is two years from the date you discovered or ought to have discovered the claim. Filing promptly is strongly recommended once you are aware of the basis for your claim.

Q: What happens at the Settlement Conference?
A: A deputy judge meets with both parties to explore settlement and simplify the issues. Bring all your evidence and be prepared to discuss a reasonable resolution. Cases that do not settle at the conference proceed to a trial date.

Q: How do I collect if I win in Ontario Small Claims Court?
A: A judgment does not guarantee payment. Collection tools in Ontario include a Notice of Examination (examining the debtor under oath about their finances), garnishment of wages or bank accounts, and writs of seizure and sale of personal property. The court provides enforcement forms on its website.

Q: Can I appeal an Ontario small claims decision?
A: Yes. Either party may appeal a final order of the Small Claims Court to the Divisional Court within 30 days of the order. Appeals are limited to questions of law; factual findings by the deputy judge are generally not overturned on appeal.

Q: What if the dispute involves a French-speaking party?
A: Ontario is a bilingual province and parties have the right to use French in Small Claims Court proceedings in designated bilingual courts. The court's forms are available in both English and French.
