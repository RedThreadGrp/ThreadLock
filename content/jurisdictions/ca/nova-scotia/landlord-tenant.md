<!-- SECTION: overview -->
Nova Scotia Residential Tenancies Program (Service Nova Scotia) handles residential landlord-tenant disputes under Canadian law. Both landlords and tenants may file applications here — landlords typically seek eviction or rent repayment, while tenants seek return of security deposits, enforcement of habitability obligations, or protection from unlawful actions. Common disputes include eviction for nonpayment, illegal rent increases, security deposit disputes, habitability failures, and tenancy termination. Nova Scotia limits annual rent increases, making disputes about unauthorized rent increases a significant category of applications. Nova Scotia introduced rent increase caps in 2020. Rent increases are limited to the annual guideline set by the province. The Residential Tenancies Program resolves disputes through mediation and hearings without requiring court involvement. <!-- SECTION: who_can_file -->
Both individual landlords and business entities (corporations, property management companies) may file with the tribunal. Tenants may also file applications to enforce their rights, recover deposits, or challenge improper actions. Security deposits in Nova Scotia are capped at 0.5 months' rent. You must file with the tribunal that has jurisdiction over the property location. If you are the tenant, you may also contact a tenant rights organization or legal aid office in Nova Scotia before filing to understand your options. Fee waivers may be available for low-income filers — ask the tribunal clerk or office.

<!-- SECTION: process -->
Pre-filing notice requirement: 15 days for nonpayment; 1 month for substantial breach. This notice must be properly served — typically by hand delivery, certified mail, or posting on the door — and the notice period must fully expire before you file with the tribunal. Failure to give proper notice is a common reason cases are dismissed.

To begin a dispute in Nova Scotia, file an application with the Nova Scotia Residential Tenancies Program (Service Nova Scotia). The filing fee is $35–$75. If you are a tenant filing against a landlord (for deposit return, habitability, or unlawful entry), the process is similar — complete the appropriate form and pay the applicable fee.

The tribunal will review the application, notify the other party, and schedule a hearing or mediation session. Many Canadian residential tenancy tribunals conduct hearings by phone or video. Bring all relevant documents — lease, rent receipts, communications, photos — to the hearing.

The tribunal can issue a variety of orders: payment of rent arrears, return of a security deposit, possession of the rental unit, or compensation for damages. Tribunal orders are legally enforceable. Security deposit: landlords in Nova Scotia must return the deposit within 10 days after move-out. Failure to meet this deadline may forfeit the landlord's right to make any deductions, depending on the jurisdiction.

<!-- SECTION: evidence_and_prep -->
Landlord-tenant disputes are won or lost on documentation. The Nova Scotia Residential Tenancies Program (Service Nova Scotia) expects organized, chronological evidence — not narrative arguments. Whatever the dispute type, the starting point is always the same: the lease agreement, the rental payment history, and the written communications between landlord and tenant.

For Nova Scotia disputes involving eviction for nonpayment, illegal rent increases, security deposit disputes, habitability failures, and tenancy termination, the evidence that most often determines outcomes includes: dated photographs of property condition at move-in and move-out, text message and email records showing what was communicated and when, maintenance request logs showing when issues were reported and whether they were addressed, and copies of all notices with proof of service. Courts and tribunals cannot award what you cannot prove — bring every document that supports your position.

Q: How much notice does a landlord need to give before evicting a tenant in Nova Scotia?
A: The required notice depends on the reason. For nonpayment of rent: 15 days for nonpayment; 1 month for substantial breach. A landlord who files without giving proper notice first may have the case dismissed. The notice must be properly served — not just sent — and the full notice period must expire before filing.

Q: How long does a landlord have to return a security deposit in Nova Scotia?
A: 10 days after move-out. If the landlord misses this deadline or fails to provide an itemized statement of deductions, they may lose the right to withhold any portion of the deposit. Security deposits in Nova Scotia are capped at 0.5 months' rent.

Q: What happens if a tenant does not leave after receiving a notice to quit?
A: If the tenant does not vacate or remedy the issue within the notice period, the landlord may file an eviction application with the Nova Scotia Residential Tenancies Program (Service Nova Scotia). If the tribunal grants possession, the landlord receives a writ that allows law enforcement to remove the tenant if they still do not leave voluntarily. A landlord may not change locks, remove belongings, or shut off utilities to force a tenant out — that is an illegal lockout regardless of the circumstances.

Q: What can a tenant do if the landlord fails to make necessary repairs?
A: Tenants in Nova Scotia have the right to a habitable rental unit. If a landlord fails to make repairs after being notified in writing, a tenant may be able to file a complaint with the Nova Scotia Residential Tenancies Program (Service Nova Scotia), withhold rent (in some jurisdictions), or pursue other remedies under Nova Scotia law. Document every repair request in writing with the date sent — this record is essential if the matter goes to tribunal.

Q: Are there limits on how much a landlord can raise rent in Nova Scotia?
A: Yes. Nova Scotia limits annual rent increases. Landlords must follow the required notice periods and stay within the applicable guideline or cap. Tenants who receive an above-guideline increase notice may challenge it through the tribunal within the applicable time limit.
