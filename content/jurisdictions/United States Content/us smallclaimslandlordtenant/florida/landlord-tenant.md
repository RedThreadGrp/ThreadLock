<!-- SECTION: overview -->
Florida County Court handles residential landlord-tenant disputes. Both landlords and tenants may file cases here — landlords typically seek eviction or rent repayment, while tenants seek return of security deposits, enforcement of habitability obligations, or protection from unlawful actions. Common disputes include eviction for nonpayment, security deposit deduction disputes, habitability and mold issues, landlord entry violations, and lease renewal disagreements. Florida landlords must send a written notice of intent to make deductions within 30 days of move-out, or they forfeit the right to withhold any portion of the deposit. <!-- SECTION: who_can_file -->
Both landlords and tenants may initiate cases. Landlords typically file eviction actions or claims for unpaid rent and property damage. Tenants file to recover security deposits, enforce habitability rights, or challenge unlawful lockouts or rent increases. Florida has no statutory cap on security deposit amounts. You must file with the court that has jurisdiction over the property location. If you are the tenant, you may also contact a tenant rights organization or legal aid office in Florida before filing to understand your options. Fee waivers may be available for low-income filers — ask the court clerk or office.

<!-- SECTION: process -->
Pre-filing notice requirement: 3 days for nonpayment; 7 days for lease violations. This notice must be properly served — typically by hand delivery, certified mail, or posting on the door — and the notice period must fully expire before you file with the court. Failure to give proper notice is a common reason cases are dismissed.

To file an eviction or landlord-tenant action in Florida, visit the Florida County Court and complete the appropriate complaint or summons form. The filing fee is $185–$400. If you are a tenant filing against a landlord (for deposit return, habitability, or unlawful entry), the process is similar — complete the appropriate form and pay the applicable fee.

After filing, the court will schedule a hearing. The other party will be served with notice. Both parties present their case to the judge — bring all supporting documents: lease, rent receipts, notices, communications, and photographs.

The judge may issue a judgment for possession (eviction), money owed, or other relief. If a judgment for possession is granted, a Writ of Possession allows law enforcement to remove the tenant if they do not vacate voluntarily. Security deposit: landlords in Florida must return the deposit within 15 days after move-out (no deductions); 30 days (with itemized deductions). Failure to meet this deadline may forfeit the landlord's right to make any deductions, depending on the jurisdiction.

<!-- SECTION: evidence_and_prep -->
Landlord-tenant disputes are won or lost on documentation. The Florida County Court expects organized, chronological evidence — not narrative arguments. Whatever the dispute type, the starting point is always the same: the lease agreement, the rental payment history, and the written communications between landlord and tenant.

For Florida disputes involving eviction for nonpayment, security deposit deduction disputes, habitability and mold issues, landlord entry violations, and lease renewal disagreements, the evidence that most often determines outcomes includes: dated photographs of property condition at move-in and move-out, text message and email records showing what was communicated and when, maintenance request logs showing when issues were reported and whether they were addressed, and copies of all notices with proof of service. Courts and tribunals cannot award what you cannot prove — bring every document that supports your position.

<!-- SECTION: faq -->
Q: How much notice does a landlord need to give before evicting a tenant in Florida?
A: The required notice depends on the reason. For nonpayment of rent: 3 days for nonpayment; 7 days for lease violations. A landlord who files without giving proper notice first may have the case dismissed. The notice must be properly served — not just sent — and the full notice period must expire before filing.

Q: How long does a landlord have to return a security deposit in Florida?
A: 15 days after move-out (no deductions); 30 days (with itemized deductions). If the landlord misses this deadline or fails to provide an itemized statement of deductions, they may lose the right to withhold any portion of the deposit. Florida does not cap the deposit amount by statute.

Q: What happens if a tenant does not leave after receiving a notice to quit?
A: If the tenant does not vacate or remedy the issue within the notice period, the landlord may file an eviction case with the Florida County Court. If the court grants possession, the landlord receives a writ that allows law enforcement to remove the tenant if they still do not leave voluntarily. A landlord may not change locks, remove belongings, or shut off utilities to force a tenant out — that is an illegal lockout regardless of the circumstances.

Q: What can a tenant do if the landlord fails to make necessary repairs?
A: Tenants in Florida have the right to a habitable rental unit. If a landlord fails to make repairs after being notified in writing, a tenant may be able to file a complaint with the Florida County Court, withhold rent (in some jurisdictions), or pursue other remedies under Florida law. Document every repair request in writing with the date sent — this record is essential if the matter goes to court.
