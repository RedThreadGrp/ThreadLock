<!-- SECTION: overview -->
Massachusetts Probate and Family Court handles family law matters. Cases here include divorce (1A uncontested; 1B contested), child custody and parenting plans, child support, alimony, equitable division of marital estate, and abuse prevention orders. Family court proceedings are among the most consequential civil matters a person navigates — decisions made here affect children, property, and financial stability for years. Massachusetts has a unique 120-day 'nisi period' after a divorce judgment is entered — the divorce does not become final until that period expires, giving parties time to reconcile or appeal. <!-- SECTION: who_can_file -->
Either spouse may file for divorce or family law relief in Massachusetts. Unmarried parents may also file custody and support cases without a divorce proceeding. Residency requirement: If grounds arose in Massachusetts, any Massachusetts resident may file. If grounds arose elsewhere, must be resident for 1 year.. Only one spouse needs to meet the residency requirement. Fee waivers are available for those who cannot afford the filing fee. Self-represented litigants (pro se) are permitted in Massachusetts family court. If children are involved, the court will also consider their best interests independently, regardless of what the parties agree to.

<!-- SECTION: process -->
To begin a family court case in Massachusetts, file the petition or complaint for divorce, custody, or support with the Massachusetts Probate and Family Court. The filing fee is $200–$215. After filing, serve the petition on the other party.

Waiting period: 120 days (nisi period) after divorce judgment before it becomes absolute. The respondent has 20 days after service to file a response or answer. If the respondent does not respond, you may seek a default judgment. If they respond and contested issues remain, the court will schedule conferences and hearings.

Contested matters proceed through hearings, and complex cases may go to trial. Uncontested cases — where all issues are agreed in writing — resolve significantly faster. Typical timeline from filing to resolution: 30–180 days depending on contested issues.

All agreements on custody, support, and property division must be approved by the court — parties cannot simply agree privately and consider the matter closed. Orders and decrees are legally enforceable, and violations can result in contempt proceedings.

<!-- SECTION: evidence_and_prep -->
Family court decisions depend heavily on documentation. The Massachusetts Probate and Family Court considers evidence of parenting history, financial circumstances, living conditions, and the pattern of the relationship when making orders about divorce (1A uncontested; 1B contested), child custody and parenting plans, child support, alimony, equitable division of marital estate, and abuse prevention orders.

The most important documents in family court cases include: the marriage certificate, financial statements (income, assets, debts), tax returns, property records, communications between parties (emails, texts), records of parenting time and exchanges, documentation of any incidents of domestic violence or substance abuse, and evidence of each parent's relationship with the children.

The most overlooked evidence in custody and parenting cases is the contemporaneous record — notes written at the time of an incident, not reconstructed afterward. Courts give more weight to records that were created in real time than to summaries written later. Q: How long must I live in Massachusetts before I can file for divorce?
A: If grounds arose in Massachusetts, any Massachusetts resident may file. If grounds arose elsewhere, must be resident for 1 year.. Filing before meeting this requirement will result in the court dismissing your case for lack of jurisdiction. Only one spouse needs to meet the residency requirement.

Q: How long does the divorce process take in Massachusetts?
A: Waiting period: 120 days (nisi period) after divorce judgment before it becomes absolute. After that, an uncontested divorce can often finalize quickly if all issues are agreed. Contested divorces involving children or disputed property can take significantly longer.

Q: How does Massachusetts determine child custody?
A: Massachusetts family courts use the best interests of the child standard. Factors include each parent's relationship with the child, the child's current living situation and stability, each parent's ability to meet the child's needs, and the child's own preferences (depending on age and maturity). Courts generally prefer arrangements that allow children to maintain relationships with both parents unless safety concerns require otherwise.

Q: How is property divided in a Massachusetts divorce?
A: Massachusetts uses equitable distribution — courts divide marital property in a way that is fair, which does not always mean equal. Factors include the length of the marriage, each spouse's income and financial contributions, and the economic circumstances of each party after divorce. Property owned before the marriage or received as a gift or inheritance may be treated as separate.

Q: What does it cost to file for divorce in Massachusetts?
A: The court filing fee is $200–$215. This does not include attorney fees, mediation costs, or other court fees that may apply. Fee waivers are available for those who qualify based on financial hardship — ask the court clerk for the application.
