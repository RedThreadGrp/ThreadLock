<!-- SECTION: overview -->
New York Small Claims Court is a division of the Civil Court in New York City and the District or City Courts outside the five boroughs. It handles civil money disputes up to $10,000 and is intentionally informal so that individuals can represent themselves without legal training. Common cases include security deposit disputes, unpaid loans, property damage, and minor contract breaches. <!-- SECTION: who_can_file -->
Any individual 18 or older may use small claims court in New York. Businesses may also file, but corporations, partnerships, and associations must be represented by an officer or authorized representative — not outside counsel — at the hearing. Cases must be filed in the court located in the county or borough where the defendant lives or does business. New York City has small claims parts in each of the five boroughs; outside the city, cases are filed in District Court, City Court, or Town/Village Justice Court depending on the location.

<!-- SECTION: process -->
The plaintiff files a claim by going to the small claims clerk's office and completing the claim form. The filing fee is $15 for claims up to $1,000 and $20 for claims between $1,000 and $10,000. The court then mails a notice to the defendant with the hearing date and time — there is no requirement for the defendant to file a formal written response before the hearing.

Hearings in New York small claims court are typically held in the evening to accommodate working claimants. Both parties appear before a judge or arbitrator and present their case informally. If both parties consent, the case may be heard by a trained volunteer arbitrator, which can result in a faster resolution. Decisions by arbitrators are final and cannot be appealed; decisions by judges may be appealed.

If the defendant does not appear, the judge may enter a default judgment. If the plaintiff does not appear, the case may be dismissed. Judgments do not automatically result in payment — the winner must take additional steps such as income execution (wage garnishment) or bank account restraint to collect.

<!-- SECTION: evidence_and_prep -->
Effective evidence in New York small claims court includes written contracts, receipts, invoices, canceled checks, bank statements, photographs, text messages, and email correspondence. <!-- SECTION: faq -->
Q: Do I need an attorney for New York small claims court?
A: No. Small claims court is designed for self-represented individuals. Attorneys are permitted, but many claimants handle their own cases. For claims under a few thousand dollars, the cost of an attorney often exceeds the potential recovery.

Q: What is the difference between using a judge and an arbitrator?
A: Cases decided by arbitrators are resolved faster and the process is more informal, but the decision is final and cannot be appealed. Cases before a judge may be appealed to the Appellate Term. If you are unsure whether you have grounds for appeal, consider opting for a judge.

Q: How does the defendant find out about my claim?
A: The court mails a notice to the defendant at the address you provide. There is no formal service by sheriff in most small claims cases. Make sure you have a current and accurate address for the defendant.

Q: Can I collect my judgment automatically once I win?
A: No. Winning a judgment is the first step. To actually receive payment, you may need to file for income execution (wage garnishment) or a bank levy. The court provides information on collection procedures, or you can consult the New York Courts self-help center.

Q: What happens if I filed in the wrong court location?
A: The case may be transferred to the correct court, or the defendant may object to venue. Filing in the correct location — where the defendant lives or does business — avoids delays and possible dismissal.
