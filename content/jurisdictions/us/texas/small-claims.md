<!-- SECTION: overview -->
Texas small claims cases are handled in Justice Courts, which have jurisdiction over civil disputes up to $20,000 — one of the highest limits in the country. Justice Courts follow simplified rules of procedure designed for non-lawyers. Common cases include unpaid debts, property damage, security deposit disputes, and contract disagreements. <!-- SECTION: who_can_file -->
Any individual, business, or government entity may file a small claims case in Texas Justice Court. There is no restriction on attorney representation — either party may have an attorney, though many people handle small claims cases without one given the simplified procedures. The case must be filed in the precinct where the defendant resides, where the defendant has a principal place of business, or where the incident or contract at issue occurred. Texas has 254 counties, each with multiple justice court precincts.

<!-- SECTION: process -->
To file a small claims case in Texas, the plaintiff completes a Petition (Form SC-1) and files it with the justice court in the appropriate precinct. Filing fees range from $46 for claims under $10,000 to approximately $140 for claims approaching the $20,000 limit, though fees vary slightly by county. The court issues a citation directing the defendant to appear or respond.

The defendant must file a written answer within 14 days of being served. If the defendant does not answer, the plaintiff may request a default judgment. Texas allows counterclaims in small claims proceedings, so defendants should review the petition carefully before the hearing.

At the hearing, both parties present evidence and testimony directly to the justice of the peace or judge. Rules of evidence are relaxed, and the judge may ask questions to clarify the dispute. Judgments are usually entered the same day. Losing parties have 21 days to file a motion for new trial or to appeal to the county court. Appeals are de novo — the case is retried from scratch.

<!-- SECTION: evidence_and_prep -->
Key evidence in Texas Justice Court cases includes written agreements, invoices, receipts, photographs, videos, text messages, emails, and any records of payments made or demanded. Texas judges appreciate concise, fact-focused presentations. Bring originals plus copies for the judge and the opposing party.

<!-- SECTION: faq -->
Q: Can an attorney represent me in Texas Justice Court?
A: Yes. Unlike some states, Texas permits attorneys in small claims proceedings. However, many claimants handle these cases themselves given the simplified rules.

Q: What is the statute of limitations for small claims in Texas?
A: Most contract claims must be filed within four years. Injury or property damage claims generally have a two-year limitation. Filing promptly is advisable to preserve evidence and witness memory.

Q: How is the defendant notified about my claim?
A: The court issues a citation that is served on the defendant by a constable or sheriff, or by certified mail. You are responsible for providing the defendant's correct address at the time of filing.

Q: Can I appeal a Texas Justice Court judgment?
A: Yes. Either party may appeal to the county court within 21 days of the judgment. The appeal is a new trial (de novo), and the county court's decision is final except in unusual circumstances.

Q: What if I cannot locate the defendant to serve them?
A: If the defendant cannot be located by ordinary means, you may petition for service by publication or other alternative methods, but this adds time and complexity to the case. The court clerk can advise you on the procedure.
