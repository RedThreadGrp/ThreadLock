<!-- SECTION: overview -->
Florida small claims court operates within the County Court system and handles civil money disputes up to $8,000. The process is designed for self-represented individuals and follows the Florida Small Claims Rules, which are simplified compared to the general civil rules. Common cases include unpaid debts, landlord-tenant security deposit disputes, property damage, and contract disagreements. <!-- SECTION: who_can_file -->
Any individual, business, or entity may file a small claims case in Florida County Court. Corporations must generally be represented by a licensed attorney unless they qualify for the self-represented corporate officer exception. The case must be filed in the county where the defendant resides, where the defendant's principal place of business is located, or where the cause of action arose. Florida has 67 counties, each with its own clerk of court.

<!-- SECTION: process -->
To begin a Florida small claims case, the plaintiff files a Statement of Claim (Form 7.322) with the county court clerk. Filing fees are $55 for claims up to $100, $80 for claims up to $500, $175 for claims up to $2,500, and $300 for claims up to $8,000. The clerk's office schedules a pretrial conference and serves the defendant.

Florida's small claims process includes a mandatory pretrial conference before the actual trial. At the pretrial conference, the judge or hearing officer encourages the parties to settle. If no settlement is reached, the case is set for trial. The defendant must respond within 20 days of service, though the pretrial conference notice serves as the primary trigger for the defendant's engagement.

At trial, both parties present evidence and testimony before the judge. Florida small claims rules allow the judge to accept any relevant evidence. Judgments may be entered at the hearing or mailed shortly afterward. The losing party has 30 days to appeal to the circuit court.

<!-- SECTION: evidence_and_prep -->
Florida judges in small claims court expect parties to come prepared with all relevant documents. Useful evidence includes written agreements, receipts, photographs, repair estimates, text messages, emails, and any records showing the history of payments or demands. Florida's pretrial conference means there are two court appearances — preparation for both is important.

<!-- SECTION: faq -->
Q: What is the purpose of the pretrial conference in Florida small claims?
A: The pretrial conference allows the judge or mediator to help the parties explore settlement before going to trial. Many cases settle at this stage, avoiding the need for a full hearing. You should arrive prepared to discuss settlement but also ready to proceed to trial if no agreement is reached.

Q: Can I bring witnesses to Florida small claims court?
A: Yes. Witnesses may testify at trial. You should notify your witnesses of the hearing date in advance. If a witness is unwilling to appear voluntarily, you may request a subpoena from the clerk's office.

Q: What is the filing deadline for small claims in Florida?
A: The statute of limitations depends on the type of claim. Written contract disputes typically must be filed within five years; oral contracts within four years; property damage within four years; and personal injury within two years. Check the specific limitation period for your type of claim.

Q: How are Florida small claims judgments collected?
A: Winning a judgment does not result in automatic payment. You may pursue collection through wage garnishment, bank account levies, or property liens. Florida has certain exemptions that protect a portion of wages and assets from garnishment.

Q: Can I represent a business in Florida small claims court?
A: Generally, corporations and LLCs must be represented by a licensed attorney in Florida courts. However, a sole proprietor or individual doing business under a trade name may appear without an attorney. Verify the rule with the clerk's office before filing.
