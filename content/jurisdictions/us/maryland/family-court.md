<!-- SECTION: overview -->
Maryland Circuit Court handles family law matters. Cases here include absolute divorce, limited divorce (legal separation), child custody, child support, alimony, property division, and domestic violence protective orders. Family court proceedings are among the most consequential civil matters a person navigates — decisions made here affect children, property, and financial stability for years. Maryland requires spouses to live physically separate and apart for a period of time before a no-fault divorce can be granted. Maryland updated its divorce laws in 2023. For mutual consent divorce, there is no waiting period if parties have a written settlement. For no-fault without consent, 6 months separation is required. Fault grounds remain available. Maryland uses equitable distribution. <!-- SECTION: who_can_file -->
Either spouse may file for divorce or family law relief in Maryland. Unmarried parents may also file custody and support cases without a divorce proceeding. Residency requirement: No minimum — may file if grounds occurred in Maryland or parties were married in Maryland; or 1 year separation if grounds arose elsewhere. Only one spouse needs to meet the residency requirement. Fee waivers are available for those who cannot afford the filing fee. Self-represented litigants (pro se) are permitted in Maryland family court. If children are involved, the court will also consider their best interests independently, regardless of what the parties agree to.

<!-- SECTION: process -->
Important: before filing for no-fault divorce in Maryland, the separation requirement must be met (6 months to 1 year separation depending on grounds and whether parties agree). You may file custody, support, and protective order cases separately while the separation period runs. To begin a family court case in Maryland, file the petition or complaint for divorce, custody, or support with the Maryland Circuit Court. The filing fee is $165. After filing, serve the petition on the other party.

Waiting period: 6 months to 1 year separation depending on grounds and whether parties agree. The respondent has 30 days after service to file a response or answer. If the respondent does not respond, you may seek a default judgment. If they respond and contested issues remain, the court will schedule conferences and hearings.

Contested matters proceed through hearings, and complex cases may go to trial. Uncontested cases — where all issues are agreed in writing — resolve significantly faster. Typical timeline from filing to resolution: 60–180 days after filing.

All agreements on custody, support, and property division must be approved by the court — parties cannot simply agree privately and consider the matter closed. Orders and decrees are legally enforceable, and violations can result in contempt proceedings.

<!-- SECTION: evidence_and_prep -->
Family court decisions depend heavily on documentation. The Maryland Circuit Court considers evidence of parenting history, financial circumstances, living conditions, and the pattern of the relationship when making orders about absolute divorce, limited divorce (legal separation), child custody, child support, alimony, property division, and domestic violence protective orders.

The most important documents in family court cases include: the marriage certificate, financial statements (income, assets, debts), tax returns, property records, communications between parties (emails, texts), records of parenting time and exchanges, documentation of any incidents of domestic violence or substance abuse, and evidence of each parent's relationship with the children.

Q: How long must I live in Maryland before I can file for divorce?
A: No minimum — may file if grounds occurred in Maryland or parties were married in Maryland; or 1 year separation if grounds arose elsewhere. Filing before meeting this requirement will result in the court dismissing your case for lack of jurisdiction. Only one spouse needs to meet the residency requirement.

Q: Do I have to be separated before filing for divorce in Maryland?
A: Yes. 6 months to 1 year separation depending on grounds and whether parties agree. You may file custody, child support, and protection order cases during the separation period — you do not have to wait for the full period before addressing those issues. Only the final divorce decree is held until the requirement is met.

Q: How does Maryland determine child custody?
A: Maryland family courts use the best interests of the child standard. Factors include each parent's relationship with the child, the child's current living situation and stability, each parent's ability to meet the child's needs, and the child's own preferences (depending on age and maturity). Courts generally prefer arrangements that allow children to maintain relationships with both parents unless safety concerns require otherwise.

Q: How is property divided in a Maryland divorce?
A: Maryland uses equitable distribution — courts divide marital property in a way that is fair, which does not always mean equal. Factors include the length of the marriage, each spouse's income and financial contributions, and the economic circumstances of each party after divorce. Property owned before the marriage or received as a gift or inheritance may be treated as separate.

Q: What does it cost to file for divorce in Maryland?
A: The court filing fee is $165. This does not include attorney fees, mediation costs, or other court fees that may apply. Fee waivers are available for those who qualify based on financial hardship — ask the court clerk for the application.
