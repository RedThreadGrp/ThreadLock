<!-- SECTION: overview -->
Oregon small claims court handles civil disputes up to $10,000 without requiring an attorney. It is designed for everyday disputes such as unpaid loans, property damage, security deposit disagreements, and small contract breaches. The process is deliberately simplified so that ordinary people can navigate it without legal training. <!-- SECTION: who_can_file -->
Any individual, business, or corporation may file a small claims case in Oregon. However, corporations and LLCs must be represented by a corporate officer — not outside counsel — when appearing in small claims court. The case must be filed in the county where the defendant lives or does business, or where the contract was performed. There is no residency requirement for the plaintiff, but venue rules apply.

<!-- SECTION: process -->
To begin a small claims case in Oregon, the plaintiff files a Small Claims Complaint form (OJD SC-01) at the circuit court clerk's office in the appropriate county. The filing fee ranges from $52 for claims under $750 to $95 for claims up to $10,000. Once filed, the court schedules a hearing date and the defendant is served with a copy of the complaint and a hearing notice.

The defendant has 14 days from service to file a written response using form OJD SC-02. If the defendant does not respond, the plaintiff may request a default judgment. At the hearing, both parties present their case directly to the judge or magistrate. No formal rules of evidence apply, but the judge will ask each side to explain their position and present any supporting documents.

After the hearing, the judge issues a judgment. If the plaintiff wins, the judgment is entered in the court record. Collecting the judgment is a separate process — the court does not collect money on the plaintiff's behalf. Winning parties may pursue wage garnishment, bank levies, or property liens to collect.

<!-- SECTION: evidence_and_prep -->
The most useful evidence in Oregon small claims cases includes written contracts or agreements, payment records, receipts, photographs of damage, text messages or emails documenting the dispute, and bank statements. Judges appreciate organized, chronological presentation — a clear timeline showing what happened and when is more persuasive than a stack of unorganized papers.

<!-- SECTION: faq -->
Q: Can I file a small claims case without an attorney?
A: Yes. Oregon small claims court is specifically designed for self-represented litigants. Attorneys are permitted but rarely used for claims under $2,500, and many judges prefer the simpler format of pro se presentations.

Q: What happens if the defendant does not show up?
A: The court may enter a default judgment in your favor. You will still need to present your evidence and state the amount you are seeking, as the judge does not automatically award the full claim amount.

Q: How do I collect my judgment if I win?
A: Winning a judgment does not guarantee payment. You may need to garnish wages, place a lien on property, or pursue bank account levies. Oregon courts provide information on collection procedures at the time of judgment.

Q: Can I appeal a small claims decision?
A: Yes. Either party may appeal the decision to the circuit court within 10 days of the judgment. The appeal is heard as a new trial (de novo), so both parties present their case again to a circuit court judge.

Q: What is the filing deadline for small claims in Oregon?
A: Oregon's statute of limitations for most contract and property damage claims is six years. However, you should file as soon as possible to preserve evidence and witness availability.
