<!-- SECTION: overview -->
California small claims court resolves civil money disputes up to $12,500 for individuals and $6,250 for businesses without requiring an attorney. Attorneys are not permitted to represent parties at the hearing. Common cases include unpaid loans, security deposit disputes, property damage, and minor contract breaches. California's streamlined process makes it one of the most accessible courts for self-represented litigants in the country. <!-- SECTION: who_can_file -->
Any individual 18 or older may file in California small claims court. Minors must have a guardian ad litem file on their behalf. Businesses, partnerships, and corporations may file up to the $6,250 limit. Attorneys may not appear at the hearing on behalf of a party, though parties may consult with an attorney before or after the hearing. Cases must be filed in the court district where the defendant lives, where the defendant's business is located, or where the contract was signed or to be performed.

<!-- SECTION: process -->
The plaintiff begins by filing form SC-100 (Plaintiff's Claim) at the small claims division of the superior court in the appropriate county. Filing fees are $30 for claims up to $1,500, $50 for claims up to $5,000, and $75–$100 for claims up to $12,500. If you have filed more than 12 small claims cases in a calendar year, higher fees apply.

After filing, the plaintiff is responsible for serving the defendant at least 15 days before the hearing date if the defendant is in California. The defendant may file a Defendant's Claim (SC-120) if they have a counterclaim. At the hearing, both parties present their case directly to the judge. Judgments are typically issued at the hearing or mailed within a few days.

California requires the losing party to wait 30 days before the winner can begin collections, to allow time for appeal. If no appeal is filed, the judgment becomes enforceable and the winning party may use wage garnishment, bank levies, or property liens to collect.

Strong evidence in California small claims cases includes written contracts, invoices, receipts, photographs, text messages, emails, and witness statements. California judges are experienced with self-represented litigants and value clear, organized presentations over legal arguments.

<!-- SECTION: faq -->
Q: Can an attorney appear in California small claims court?
A: No. Attorneys are prohibited from representing parties at small claims hearings. You may consult a lawyer before your hearing, but you must present your case yourself.

Q: What is the statute of limitations for small claims in California?
A: It depends on the type of claim. Written contracts have a four-year limit, oral contracts two years, and property damage three years. File promptly to avoid losing your right to sue.

Q: How do I serve the defendant?
A: You may use certified mail through the court clerk, have a sheriff serve the defendant, or use a process server. Personal service or substituted service is also permitted. The court provides instructions on proper service methods.

Q: Can I appeal a California small claims judgment?
A: Yes. Either party may appeal to the superior court within 30 days of the judgment. The appeal is a new trial. Note that if you were the plaintiff and lost, you may not appeal — only defendants may appeal a judgment against them in most situations.

Q: What happens if the defendant lives in another state?
A: You can still file in California if the contract was to be performed there or the event occurred there, but serving an out-of-state defendant may be more complex and you should consult court self-help resources.
